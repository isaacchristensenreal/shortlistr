How scoring works

What an ATS Score Actually Measures

ATS scoring isn't a mystery — it's a specific formula. These are the components that contribute to your score, ranked by how much weight they typically carry.

Keyword match in experience

How many of the job description's required keywords appear in your work experience bullets. Highest weight in the score.

Highest impact

Job title alignment

Whether your most recent title matches or closely mirrors the job title you're applying for. Often a binary pass/fail weight.

High impact

Skills section keywords

Hard skills, tools, technologies, and certifications listed in your skills section. Lower weight than experience bullets, but still scored.

Medium impact

Required qualifications triggers

Binary conditions like "5+ years experience" or "Bachelor's degree required." Missing these can be an automatic disqualifier regardless of overall score.

Binary filter

Formatting parseability

Whether the ATS can actually extract text from your resume. Tables, columns, text boxes, and graphics cause parse failures that tank your score regardless of content.

Pass/fail blocker

Education and credentials

Degree level, field of study, and certifications. Matched against any explicit education requirements in the job description.

Score ranges explained

What Each Score Range Means for Your Application

Here's exactly what happens to your resume depending on which zone your score falls in — and what's typically causing each range.

0–40

Automatically filtered — never reaches a human

A score in this range triggers automatic rejection in virtually all ATS configurations. Your application is marked as not qualifying and removed from review before any recruiter sees it. This is where the majority of generic resumes land.

Usually caused by: wrong vocabulary, missing required keywords, or broken formatting
41–70

The "maybe" pile — depends on competition

Scores in this range sometimes get through — particularly for roles with fewer applicants or at smaller companies with lower ATS threshold settings. But at high-volume employers, 70% is typically the floor for human review. You're competing against candidates at 80–90% for the same recruiter's attention.

Usually caused by: partial keyword matches, missing preferred qualifications
71–100

Passes ATS — your resume reaches a real person

A score of 71%+ reliably passes ATS filters across most employer configurations. At 85%+, you're in the top tier of applicants for that role. Your resume now competes on its actual merit — work history, achievements, and presentation — rather than being filtered before anyone reads it.

Getting here: tailor keywords, fix formatting, mirror job description language

The average tailored resume scores 72–84% on its target job. The average untailored resume scores 31–47%. That gap is entirely explainable by keyword alignment — and entirely fixable in under 60 seconds with Shortlistr.

How to Move Your Score Into the Green Zone Fast

These are the highest-impact changes you can make to your resume — ordered by how much each one typically moves your score.

1

Replace your vocabulary with the job description's vocabulary

This is the single biggest lever. Find every required skill, tool, and qualification in the job posting and rewrite your experience bullets to use the exact same phrasing. "Led cross-functional teams" instead of "managed multiple groups." Same experience — different ATS score.

⚡ Typical score boost: +20 to +35 points
2

Move keywords from your skills list into experience bullets

ATS systems weight keywords in your experience section significantly more than in a standalone skills list. If "Salesforce" is in your skills section but not in any bullet describing where you used it, rewrite one bullet to include it in context.

⚡ Typical score boost: +8 to +15 points
3

Remove tables, columns, and graphics

Multi-column layouts, text boxes, headers, and graphics cause ATS parsers to jumble or skip your content entirely. Convert to a clean single-column format. Your score can't be calculated if your text can't be read.

⚡ Typical score boost: +10 to +25 points (if formatting was broken)
4

Use standard section headings

ATS expects "Work Experience," "Education," and "Skills." Non-standard headings like "My Story," "What I Bring," or "Career Highlights" cause the system to misclassify or skip those sections in scoring.

⚡ Typical score boost: +5 to +12 points
5

Add your target job title to your resume headline

If applying for "Senior Product Manager," your resume headline or summary should include that exact phrase. Title matching is typically a high-weight signal in ATS scoring and takes 30 seconds to fix.

⚡ Typical score boost: +5 to +10 points
6

Run your score with Shortlistr before every application

Once you've made manual improvements, scan your resume against each specific job before submitting. Different jobs use different keywords — a resume tailored for one role may still score 45% on a similar role at a different company.

Common questions

Frequently Asked Questions

  • What is an ATS score?
    An ATS score is the percentage match between your resume and a specific job description, as calculated by an Applicant Tracking System. It measures how many of the job's required keywords, skills, and qualifications appear in your resume. Scores below 60% typically result in automatic rejection before a human reads your application.
  • What is a good ATS score?
    A score of 70% or higher is generally considered strong enough to pass ATS filters and reach a human recruiter. Scores of 85%+ are excellent and significantly increase your chances of interview callbacks. Most generic resumes score in the 30–50% range on any given job posting.
  • How is an ATS score calculated?
    ATS systems compare the text of your resume against the job description using keyword matching algorithms. They look for exact and near-exact matches of required skills, job titles, tools, technologies, and qualifications. Keywords found in your experience section are typically weighted more heavily than those in a skills list.

Remove tables, columns, and graphics that ATS parsers can't read. Use standard section headings.
