What is a good ATS score? The direct answer: 80 or above is strong, placing you in the shortlisted range at most companies. Scores below 60 are almost always filtered out automatically — before a human recruiter ever sees your application. The range between 60 and 79 is a gray zone where you may technically clear the threshold but rank so low in the queue that your application never gets reviewed before the role is filled.

ATS scoring is not a single universal system — each platform calculates it differently. But the 0–100 scale is a useful common framework, and the thresholds above hold reasonably well across all major platforms. Understanding what each score range means in practice — and what causes scores to land where they do — is the key to systematically improving your callback rate.

ShortListr data point: Users who optimize their resume to score above 80 before submitting receive 3.2× more interview callbacks than those who submit unoptimized resumes. The difference is not qualifications — it is keyword coverage, placement, and formatting. All fixable. All measurable.

How ATS Scores Are Calculated

ATS scoring methodology varies by platform, but the core calculation follows a consistent logic across most systems. Understanding the formula helps you make smarter optimization decisions.

Step 1: Keyword Identification

When a recruiter creates a job posting, the ATS automatically identifies candidate keywords from the job description — or the recruiter manually adds them. These keywords are the scoring criteria. They include hard skills, tool names, certifications, job titles, methodologies, and sometimes soft skills. The more frequently a keyword appears in the job description, the higher its weight in the scoring algorithm.

Step 2: Keyword Matching

The ATS compares your parsed resume text against its keyword list. Most systems score exact matches fully, partial matches (near-synonyms) at a fraction of full value, and complete misses as zero. Older platforms like Taleo and iCIMS are largely exact-match only. Newer platforms like Greenhouse and SmartRecruiters apply some semantic matching, giving partial credit for close equivalents.

Step 3: Placement Weighting

Not all keyword locations carry equal weight. The multipliers vary by platform, but the general priority order is: professional title and summary (highest), recent role experience bullets (high), older role bullets (medium), skills section (medium), education section (low). A keyword in your current role's first bullet is worth significantly more than the same keyword buried in a role from five years ago.

Step 4: Hard Requirements Gate

Before keyword scoring is even applied, many ATS systems apply hard pass/fail gates for required qualifications: years of experience ("requires 5+ years"), degree level ("requires Bachelor's degree"), required certifications, and sometimes location. If your resume does not pass these gates, it may be disqualified regardless of keyword score. These are the non-negotiable filters you cannot keyword-optimize your way around.

Step 5: Composite Score

The system combines keyword match percentage, placement weights, and any additional factors (completeness of contact information, presence of standard resume sections) into a final composite score. The threshold for advancement — typically 60–75% depending on company settings — is set by the recruiter when they configure the job posting.

The 5 Biggest Factors That Drop Your ATS Score

Most score problems trace back to a small number of root causes. Here are the five that account for the vast majority of low ATS scores.

  • 1
    Missing Critical Keywords The most common and most impactful score killer. The job description contains specific terminology — tool names, methodologies, certifications, exact skill phrases — that your resume simply does not include. Each missing tier-1 keyword typically drops your score by 3–8 points depending on its weight. A resume missing 8–10 critical keywords moves from 85 to 55 purely from these omissions.
  • 2
    ATS-Breaking Formatting Multi-column layouts, text boxes, tables containing content, graphics, and non-standard section headers cause parsing failures that drop scores dramatically. When the parser scrambles your resume's structure, your keywords end up in the wrong fields or are dropped entirely. A resume with perfect keywords but broken formatting can score lower than a simpler, well-formatted resume with fewer keywords.
  • 3
    Paraphrased Keywords Instead of Exact Matches If the job description says "stakeholder management" and your resume says "managing relationships with stakeholders," older ATS systems score that as a zero — not a partial credit. Every tier-1 keyword that you describe using synonymous but different language is a missed scoring opportunity. The fix is straightforward: use the exact phrase the job description uses, placed naturally in your bullet points.
  • 4
    Keywords in Low-Weight Sections Only If a critical keyword appears only in your skills list or in a role from six years ago, its score contribution is a fraction of what it would be if it appeared in your summary or current role bullets. Keyword placement is as important as keyword presence. A keyword buried at the bottom of the document scores far lower than the same keyword in the first three sentences of your resume.
  • 5
    No Professional Summary Resumes without a professional summary miss the highest-weighted keyword section in the document. The summary is the first section the ATS processes and carries the largest per-keyword weight multiplier. A resume without a summary is leaving its most valuable scoring real estate empty — and is typically scoring 10–15 points lower than it would with a well-crafted, keyword-rich summary.

How to Raise Your Score From Under 60 to Above 80

The good news: the gap between a score below 60 and a score above 80 is almost entirely fixable. Here is the sequence of actions that moves the needle most efficiently, in order of impact.

  1. Fix your formatting first. If your resume has a multi-column layout, text boxes, or a design-tool-generated PDF, fix the structure before doing anything else. Keyword optimization on a broken-format resume is wasted effort — the parser cannot read the keywords even if they are there.
  2. Add a professional summary if you don't have one. A 3–4 sentence summary that includes your 5–6 most critical keywords moves your score significantly by populating the highest-weighted section in your resume.
  3. Identify tier-1 keywords from the job description. Read the Requirements and Responsibilities sections carefully. Highlight every noun that appears twice or more — these are the keywords with the highest ATS weight. Build a list of the 8–12 most critical ones.
  4. Add missing tier-1 keywords to your summary. For each keyword that is genuinely applicable to your background, work it into your professional summary naturally. One well-crafted summary can close 3–5 keyword gaps simultaneously.
  5. Rewrite 3–5 bullets in your most recent role. Take the remaining missing keywords and rewrite existing bullets to incorporate them in context. Use exact phrases, not paraphrases. Keep the achievements and numbers — just change the terminology.
  6. Update your skills section. Add any remaining keywords that are real skills but did not fit naturally into bullet points. Be specific: name tools by name, list certifications explicitly.
  7. Score before submitting. Use ShortListr to check your score after making changes. Confirm you are above 80 before applying.

Following these steps in sequence typically moves a resume from the 50–60 range to the 80–88 range for a well-matched candidate. The process takes 30–45 minutes manually or under 10 minutes with ShortListr's automated analysis and rewriting tools.

Does a Perfect Score Actually Matter?

No. Optimizing beyond 80 has diminishing returns — and can actually hurt you if pursued too aggressively. Here is why.

A score of 82 and a score of 96 produce nearly identical outcomes from the recruiter's perspective. Both land in the "reviewed" pile. Both get 7 seconds of human attention. The difference in callback rate between an 82 and a 96 is not statistically meaningful in ShortListr's data. The significant threshold is 80, not 90 or 95.

More importantly, trying to push from 82 to 96 often means adding keywords with lower relevance, increasing keyword density to the point where modern ATS spam filters start detecting manipulation, or cramming terms into bullet points where they do not fit naturally. A keyword-stuffed resume may score 94 in a tool — but if a human reads it and it sounds like a keyword list, it goes to the rejection pile instantly.

The right goal is: above 80, naturally. If you get there without forcing unnatural language, your resume serves both the ATS and the human reviewer effectively. That is the optimization sweet spot.
