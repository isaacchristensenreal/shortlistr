Here is something that should make you furious: the majority of ATS rejections have nothing to do with whether you're qualified for the job. They happen because a resume was formatted in a way the software couldn't read — a two-column layout that scrambled the parser, a PDF exported from Canva that contained no extractable text, a contact section placed inside a document header that the system silently skipped. The candidate was qualified. The software just couldn't tell.

Formatting a resume for ATS is not the same as making it look good. These are sometimes opposite goals. The formatting choices that make a resume visually impressive to a human — columns, icons, graphics, creative fonts, color-coded sections — are often the exact choices that cause ATS systems to fail silently. The software doesn't tell you it couldn't parse your resume. It just scores your application low and moves on.

The core rule: An ATS-friendly resume prioritizes parsability over aesthetics. Plain, predictable structure always beats clever design. If a recruiter printed your resume on a black-and-white office printer with no formatting, it should still read perfectly — that's the standard the ATS is applying.

This guide covers every formatting decision that affects ATS parsing: layout, fonts, file format, section naming, what to remove, and how to verify that your resume will actually parse correctly before you submit it.

The ATS-Safe Resume Format: Every Rule

These are not preferences. Each of these rules addresses a specific way ATS parsers fail. Breaking any one of them can drop your score to zero on that section — even if everything else is perfect.

✓ Do This

Single-column layout

ATS parsers read documents in a single pass from top to bottom. A single-column layout guarantees the parser reads your content in the correct order: contact → summary → experience → education → skills.

✗ Avoid This

Two-column or multi-column layouts

Parsers reading a two-column layout read horizontally across both columns simultaneously. "Python · Led cross-functional team of 12" is what the ATS sees. That matches nothing in the job description.

✓ Do This

DOCX or text-based PDF

A .docx file from Microsoft Word or a PDF exported from Word or Google Docs contains machine-readable text. The parser extracts it cleanly. This is the only format that works reliably across all ATS platforms.

✗ Avoid This

Design tool exports (Canva, InDesign)

Canva, Adobe InDesign, and similar tools embed text as image layers inside the PDF. The ATS receives a document with no readable text. Your entire resume — every keyword, every job title — is invisible to the system.

✓ Do This

Contact info in the body

Place your name, phone, email, and LinkedIn URL in the main body of the document, at the very top of page one. Keep it plain text — not a table, not a text box, not a document header element.

✗ Avoid This

Contact info in document headers/footers

Microsoft Word and Google Docs have a "Header" region — the space above the main body, typically used for page numbers. Most ATS systems skip this region entirely. Contact info placed here is parsed as blank.

✓ Do This

Standard bullet points (• or -)

Round bullets, dashes, and square bullets are universally parsed correctly. Use them consistently throughout your experience section. The content inside the bullet is what matters; the bullet character itself should be invisible to the reader.

✗ Avoid This

Tables, text boxes, or graphics

Tables are frequently skipped or scrambled by parsers. Text boxes are invisible. Images of any kind — including icons, logos, skill bar charts, and profile photos — contain zero parseable text. Remove all of them.

✓ Do This

Standard horizontal lines (---)

Use Word or Docs' built-in paragraph border for section dividers, or simply leave white space. Plain paragraph-level lines are ignored harmlessly by parsers.

✗ Avoid This

Decorative lines drawn with shapes

Lines created using the Insert → Shape tool in Word are treated as embedded graphics. Some parsers insert a parsing error character at that position, which can corrupt the text that follows it in the document.

The Resume Sections ATS Expects to Find — In This Order

ATS systems are trained to recognize specific section labels. When they encounter a section, they categorize every bullet and line inside it accordingly. A bullet in the "Work Experience" section is stored as a job entry. A bullet in the "Skills" section is stored as a skill keyword. If your section labels are unconventional, the parser either misfiles your content or ignores the section entirely.

Fonts, Sizes, and Spacing That ATS Can Actually Read

Font choice affects ATS parsing more than most people realize. ATS systems don't render your document visually — they extract the underlying text character by character. Decorative or non-standard fonts can cause characters to be replaced with encoding error symbols, corrupting the words they appear in. A keyword that contains a parsing error character matches nothing.

Font Type ATS Status Notes
Calibri Sans-serif ✓ Safe Word's default — the most universally parsed font across all ATS platforms
Arial Sans-serif ✓ Safe Clean, widely supported, no encoding issues on any platform
Garamond Serif ✓ Safe Elegant and professional — reads well for traditional industries
Georgia Serif ✓ Safe Well-spaced serif, highly readable at small sizes, universally parsed
Times New Roman Serif ✓ Safe Safe but considered dated — use Garamond or Georgia instead
Helvetica Neue Sans-serif ✓ Safe Mac-native; safe for PDF exports but may substitute on Windows parsers
Montserrat, Raleway, Lato Google Fonts ⚠ Risky Parsed correctly in most cases, but can cause encoding issues in older Taleo/iCIMS instances
Script / handwriting fonts Display ✗ Avoid Characters frequently encoded as symbols — corrupts any word using these fonts
Icon fonts (FontAwesome etc.) Icon ✗ Avoid Renders as question marks, boxes, or error characters in the parsed output

Size and spacing: Use 10–12pt for body text, 14–16pt for your name, and 11–12pt for section headers. Standard line spacing (1.0–1.15) is fine. Margins of 0.75–1 inch on all sides give parsers clean whitespace to separate sections. Never go below 0.5 inch margins — some parsers truncate text that falls too close to the document edge.

The Two-Column Resume: Why It Looks Great and Destroys Your ATS Score

This deserves its own section because two-column resume templates are everywhere — Pinterest boards, resume builder sites, Google Docs template galleries — and they look genuinely impressive. A clean two-column layout with a bold left sidebar for skills and a clean right column for experience reads well to a human eye. It solves the visual problem of fitting a lot of information onto one page. So people use them constantly, and they get silently rejected constantly.

Here's the exact mechanics of why. An ATS parser reads your PDF as a stream of text characters, moving left-to-right across the full width of the page on each horizontal line. It does not know that you've divided the page into two logical columns. It reads what's physically on the same horizontal line across the full page width.

How to Check If Your Resume Format Is ATS Compatible

Before you submit a single application, run this test. It takes three minutes and will tell you immediately whether your resume will parse correctly.

📄

Open your resume PDF

Open the PDF file you plan to submit — the exact file, not the source document. What you see in the editor may differ from what the exported PDF actually contains.

⌨️

Select all and copy (Cmd+A → Cmd+C / Ctrl+A → Ctrl+C)

Select every character in the document. If you're unable to select text — if clicking and dragging doesn't highlight anything — your PDF is image-based and ATS cannot read it at all. Stop here and rebuild in Word or Google Docs.

📝

Paste into a plain text editor (Notepad, TextEdit in plain text mode)

Paste the copied text into a bare plain-text editor. All formatting will be stripped — you're seeing exactly what an ATS parser extracts from your document.

🔍

Read the result in order

Your plain text should read logically: contact info first, then summary, then each job in reverse chronological order, then education, then skills. If sections are out of order, merged together, or if you see garbled fragments, your ATS parse is broken.

⚠️

Look for red flags

Watch for: text from the left and right columns merged into the same line, contact information missing entirely (was in document header), bullet point text mixed with skill keywords, sections appearing in the wrong order, or question marks / boxes replacing characters. Any of these means your resume will score poorly in ATS.

If your plain-text version reads cleanly and in the correct order, your formatting is ATS-safe. If it doesn't, use the rules in the previous section to identify and fix the source of the corruption before submitting.
