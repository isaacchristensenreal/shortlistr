Knowing how to write resume bullet points is the single most important skill in your entire job search. Your bullet points are the first thing a recruiter reads, the section that ATS systems parse most aggressively, and the part that determines whether you get an interview or get ignored. Yet most candidates write bullets that describe job duties instead of accomplishments — and they lose the interview before it starts.

This guide gives you the exact formula used by career coaches, recruiters, and AI tools to transform weak, duty-based bullets into compelling, quantified accomplishments. You'll get 50 action verbs organized by category, 10 before/after examples you can adapt immediately, and a clear list of the mistakes that are quietly killing your applications.

The core principle: Your resume is not a job description. It is a sales document. Every bullet point should answer the same question: "What did you achieve, and how does that achievement benefit the company that's reading this?" Duties tell recruiters what you were paid to do. Accomplishments tell them what you're capable of.

The Direct Answer: The 3-Part Bullet Formula

Every strong resume bullet follows the same structure. This formula works because it contains everything a recruiter needs to evaluate your candidacy in a single line — the action you took, the context you worked in, and the measurable outcome you delivered.

The Resume Bullet Formula
Action Verb
+
Task / Context
+
Quantified Result
Example: Reduced customer onboarding time by streamlining the verification workflow, cutting average completion from 14 days to 3 days (79% improvement) and improving Day-30 retention by 22%.

Let's break down each part in detail:

Part 1: The Action Verb

Start every bullet with a strong, past-tense action verb. Not "responsible for," not "helped with," not "worked on." A single precise verb that describes exactly what you did. The verb signals your level of ownership and leadership before the recruiter reads another word. Led is more compelling than supported. Designed is more compelling than assisted with. Choose the strongest verb that honestly represents your role.

Part 2: The Task or Context

This is the substance of the bullet — what you actually did and in what environment. Include relevant specifics: the team size, the technology, the scale, the stakeholders, the constraint. Context separates generic bullets ("managed a team") from specific, credible ones ("managed a distributed team of 11 engineers across 3 time zones building the company's core payment API"). The more specific the context, the more believable and impressive the result.

Part 3: The Quantified Result

This is the most powerful part of the bullet and the one most candidates skip. Every bullet should end with a number. A percentage. A dollar amount. A time saved. A headcount. A revenue figure. A rating. Numbers make abstract claims concrete and give recruiters a basis for comparison. "Improved conversion rate" is forgettable. "Improved conversion rate from 1.2% to 3.8% over 4 months, generating $420K in additional annual pipeline" is remembered.

Why Weak Bullet Points Kill Your Chances

Weak bullets fail on two levels simultaneously — they get filtered out by ATS systems and they fail to impress human reviewers. Understanding both failure modes helps you see why the formula matters.

How Weak Bullets Fail ATS Systems

ATS systems parse your resume looking for specific keywords, phrases, and signals. Duty-based bullets like "Responsible for managing social media accounts" tend to underuse technical keywords and contain no measurable signals. Meanwhile, a strong quantified bullet naturally introduces more keyword-rich language because it forces you to be specific: the tool you used, the methodology you applied, the metric you improved. Strong bullets pass ATS not because they're stuffed with keywords but because specificity naturally produces keyword density. To learn more about how keyword optimization works, read our guide to ATS resume keywords.

How Weak Bullets Fail Human Readers

Recruiters see hundreds of resumes per week. Duty-based bullets blend together because every candidate who held that job title had the same duties. Accomplishment-based bullets stand out immediately because they tell a story — a specific story about a specific person who achieved a specific outcome. The recruiter's job is to find the best candidate, and they can only do that if candidates give them something to compare. Generic bullets give recruiters nothing to hold onto.

The 75% problem: 75% of resumes are rejected by ATS before a human ever reads them. But even among the 25% that make it through, only the ones with compelling, evidence-based bullets get calls. Passing ATS is the minimum requirement — not the goal.

Common Bullet Point Mistakes (And How to Fix Them)

Even candidates who understand the formula often make structural mistakes that undercut otherwise strong bullets. Here are the eight most common ones and how to eliminate them.

  • 1

    Starting with "Responsible for" or "Duties included"

    These phrases immediately frame the bullet as a job description, not an accomplishment. They signal that you're telling the recruiter what you were supposed to do, not what you actually achieved. Delete them entirely and start with the action verb.

  • 2

    Using passive voice ("was tasked with," "was responsible for")

    Passive voice removes you as the agent of your own accomplishments. "Was tasked with improving customer satisfaction" puts the agency on someone else. "Improved customer satisfaction from 72% to 91% CSAT over 6 months" puts it on you. Active voice signals ownership.

  • 3

    No numbers anywhere

    If your entire resume has no percentages, dollar amounts, team sizes, or timeframes, it blends into every other candidate's resume. Every job has metrics, even if they're not obvious at first. Think about: how many people did you serve? How many units did you process? How much time did you save? How many people were on your team? Even rough estimates ("approximately 200 weekly transactions") are more credible than nothing.

  • 4

    Bullets that are three or more lines long

    Long bullets kill scannability. Recruiters skim — they don't read every word. If your bullet runs three lines, the recruiter's eye will skip to the next one. Cut ruthlessly. If a bullet contains two separate achievements, split it into two bullets. If it contains filler, delete the filler. One line, one achievement, one number.

  • 5

    Using the same verb repeatedly

    Starting five bullets in a row with "Managed" tells the recruiter you had limited range. Vary your verbs across the three categories of achievement: verbs that show leadership ("directed," "spearheaded"), verbs that show impact ("drove," "grew," "accelerated"), and verbs that show method ("analyzed," "built," "designed"). Variety signals versatility.

  • 6

    Vague, unverifiable claims

    "Significantly improved team morale." "Dramatically increased sales." "Greatly enhanced the customer experience." These bullets are unfalsifiable and therefore meaningless. Any candidate could write them. Recruiters ignore them. Replace every vague superlative with a specific number: what changed, by how much, over what timeframe.

  • 7

    Not tailoring bullets to the job description

    Using the same 15 bullets for every application is one of the most common and most costly mistakes in the job search. Each job description contains specific keywords and priorities. Your bullets need to mirror those priorities. A strong bullet about "building machine learning models" doesn't help you if the role requires "financial modeling." Customize at least 4–6 bullets per application. Read our guide on ATS keyword optimization to understand what to change.

  • 8

    Including team accomplishments without your individual contribution

    "Our team grew revenue by $5M" tells the recruiter nothing about what you did. "Contributed as lead data analyst in cross-functional team that grew revenue by $5M, personally responsible for the customer segmentation model that identified the top-performing acquisition channel" tells them exactly what your value was. Always make your individual role in team achievements explicit.

How to Find Numbers When You Think You Don't Have Any

The most common objection to the bullet formula is "I don't have numbers for my role." This is almost never true. Every job has quantifiable dimensions — you just have to know where to look.

Volume and Scale

How many? How often? How large? If you processed invoices, how many per week? If you managed clients, how many accounts? If you handled emails, roughly how many per day? Volume metrics give context to roles that seem hard to quantify.

Time and Efficiency

Did you do something faster than it was done before? Did you implement a process that saved time? Even rough estimates work: "reduced manual data entry time by approximately 4 hours per week" is more compelling than "reduced manual data entry."

Money and Cost

Did you manage a budget? Did you save the company money? Did you bring in revenue? Even indirect revenue impact counts: if you trained a sales team that then hit their quota, you can claim credit for the enablement even if not the direct sales.

People and Team Size

Did you manage people, even informally? Lead a project that involved multiple people? Train new employees? The size of teams and the scope of influence are quantifiable. "Mentored 3 junior analysts" is a number. "Led cross-functional project involving 9 stakeholders" is a number.

Ratings and Rankings

Did you receive performance reviews? Customer satisfaction scores? 360-degree feedback? Rankings within your team or region? "Ranked in the top 10% of support agents by CSAT score for 3 consecutive quarters" is a compelling, number-rich accomplishment built from data that most people forget to include.
