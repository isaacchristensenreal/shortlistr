A salary negotiation email template is the most valuable document in your job search toolkit — and most people never use one. Research consistently shows that fewer than 40% of job seekers negotiate their initial offer, despite the fact that 85% of hiring managers report having room to move on salary. The average gain from a single negotiation conversation is more than $5,000 in first-year compensation. Over a career, the compounding effect of negotiating every offer can mean hundreds of thousands of dollars in additional lifetime earnings.

The reason most people don't negotiate is simple: they don't know what to say. They're afraid of sounding greedy, damaging the relationship, or having the offer rescinded. All three fears are largely unfounded, and having a well-written email template eliminates the drafting anxiety entirely. Below are five complete, copy-and-paste salary negotiation email templates for every major scenario you'll encounter.

Before you negotiate anything: Research your market rate first. Use Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, Levels.fyi (for tech roles), the Bureau of Labor Statistics, and industry compensation surveys. Walk into (or email into) every negotiation knowing the market range for your exact role, level, and location. Negotiating without data is guessing. Negotiating with data is leverage.

When to Negotiate by Email vs. Phone

Both email and phone are appropriate for salary negotiation — the right choice depends on the context and your personal strengths.

Email is better when: You want time to craft your message carefully, you're naturally a stronger writer than speaker, the initial offer was made by email, you want a written record of what was said, or you're negotiating a raise with a current employer after submitting a formal request.

Phone (or video) is better when: The offer was extended verbally, you want to convey genuine enthusiasm alongside your counter-offer, you're in an advanced stage of a relationship-heavy process, or the negotiation involves complex tradeoffs (salary + equity + title + start date) that benefit from real-time discussion.

Many successful negotiators do both: have the initial counter-offer conversation on the phone for warmth and relationship, then follow up with an email to confirm the terms discussed. This combines the relationship benefits of a live conversation with the clarity and protection of a written record.

Template 1: Counter-Offer After Initial Job Offer

This is the most common negotiation scenario — you've received a formal offer and want to counter at a higher number. Use this template within 24–48 hours of receiving the written offer.

TEMPLATE 1 Counter-Offer After Initial Offer
Subject:Re: [Job Title] Offer — Follow-Up
Tone:Enthusiastic, professional, anchored to market data
Hi [Hiring Manager Name], Thank you so much for the offer for the [Job Title] role — I'm genuinely excited about the opportunity to join [Company Name] and contribute to [specific team/project/goal]. I've reviewed the offer carefully. Based on my [X years] of experience in [specific domain], the market rate data I've researched for this role in [location/remote], and the specific skills I'd bring to this position — particularly [2–3 key differentiators] — I was hoping we could get to [$X] in base salary. I want to be transparent that I'm very enthusiastic about this role and [Company Name] specifically. My target is [$X], but I'm open to a conversation about the full package if there's flexibility in other components like a signing bonus or additional equity. Can we connect briefly this week to discuss? I'd love to find a structure that works well for both sides. Thank you again — looking forward to talking. [Your Name]
Key principles in this template: Express genuine enthusiasm first (removes threat of rescission), anchor to market data (makes the request objective), give a specific number (vague requests are ignored), and signal flexibility in structure (opens the door for creative solutions).

Template 2: Negotiating a Raise with Your Current Employer

Asking your current employer for a raise requires a different approach than negotiating a new offer. You have more leverage (they know you), but more relationship at stake (you'll continue working together regardless of outcome). This template is designed for a formal raise request after 12+ months in role, strong performance, or a significant scope increase.

TEMPLATE 2 Raise Request — Current Employer
Subject:Request to Discuss Compensation — [Your Name]
Tone:Confident, evidence-based, forward-looking, collaborative
Hi [Manager Name], I'd like to request some time to discuss my compensation. I've given this thought carefully and I believe the conversation is timely — I wanted to come to you directly and professionally rather than letting it affect my focus or morale. Over the past [X months/year], I've taken on significantly expanded responsibility and delivered results I'm proud of. Specifically: • [Key accomplishment 1 with metric — e.g., "Led the migration to the new data platform, reducing query costs by 44% and eliminating a $180K/year vendor contract"] • [Key accomplishment 2 with metric] • [Key accomplishment 3 with metric — scope expansion, new responsibility, etc.] Based on this track record, my research into current market rates for [your title/role] in [location/remote], and the additional scope I've absorbed, I'd like to discuss bringing my base salary from [$current] to [$target] — approximately a [X%] increase. I'm committed to [Company Name] and excited about what we're building. I wanted to have this conversation now so we're aligned as we head into [next year/next quarter/the new planning cycle]. Would [day/time] work for a quick call to discuss? Thank you, [Your Name]
Key principles: Lead with the ask framing before the meeting (they'll know what it's about). Use specific bullet-pointed accomplishments — this makes the email double as a performance document. Name both your current salary and target number explicitly. Close with a proposed time to reduce scheduling friction.

Template 3: Following Up After a Verbal Negotiation

After a verbal negotiation conversation — whether on phone or video — always send a follow-up email to confirm what was discussed. This protects you, removes ambiguity, and ensures everyone is aligned before you sign.

TEMPLATE 3 Post-Verbal-Negotiation Follow-Up
Subject:Following Up on Our Conversation — [Job Title] Offer
Tone:Clear, confirmatory, warm
Hi [Hiring Manager / Recruiter Name], Thank you for taking the time to speak with me today — I appreciated the candid and productive conversation. I wanted to follow up in writing to confirm my understanding of where things stand: • Base salary: [$X] • Start date: [Date] • [Any other terms discussed — signing bonus, equity grant, remote work arrangement, title, etc.] Please let me know if I've mischaracterized anything or if there's still an outstanding item you're waiting to confirm. I'm very excited about the opportunity and am looking forward to next steps. Thank you again, [Your Name]
Why this matters: Verbal agreements get misremembered. This email creates a written record that both parties can reference. It also demonstrates professionalism and closes the loop on any open items — showing the employer you're organized and thorough.

Template 4: Negotiating Remote Work or Equity Instead of Salary

When the salary is truly non-negotiable (a firm budget band) or when you want to negotiate other components of the package, this template lets you pivot professionally to alternative forms of compensation.

TEMPLATE 4 Negotiating Equity, Remote Work, or Other Benefits
Subject:Re: [Job Title] Offer — Package Discussion
Tone:Collaborative, solution-oriented, specific about what you want
Hi [Name], Thank you for the update and for explaining the constraints around the base salary range — I understand and appreciate the transparency. I remain genuinely excited about this role and the team at [Company]. Given the salary is firm, I'd love to explore whether there's flexibility in other areas of the package that could help close the gap: 1. Signing bonus — A one-time signing bonus of [$X–$Y] would help offset the difference from my current total compensation and allow me to say yes without hesitation. 2. Equity — If there's room to increase the initial equity grant from [current amount], I'd welcome that discussion. I'm genuinely interested in a long-term relationship with [Company] and an equity stake aligns my incentives accordingly. 3. Remote flexibility — The ability to work remotely [X days per week / full remote] would be significant to me personally and would effectively increase the value of the offer considerably. I'm not looking to make this complicated — I'm excited about the opportunity and want to find a structure that makes sense for both of us. Would any of the above be possible to explore? Thank you, [Your Name]
Key principles: Acknowledging the constraint before pivoting signals you listened and you're reasonable. Numbering the alternatives makes it easy for the hiring manager to take them to their own internal chain of approval. Always close with enthusiasm to reassure them you're not walking away.

Template 5: Declining a Lowball Offer While Keeping the Door Open

Sometimes an offer is so far below your floor that accepting it doesn't make sense — but you don't want to permanently close the door with the company. This template handles the decline gracefully.

TEMPLATE 5 Graceful Decline of a Lowball Offer
Subject:[Job Title] Offer — Decision
Tone:Grateful, professional, leaves door open
Hi [Name], Thank you so much for the offer and for the time your team invested throughout the interview process — I genuinely enjoyed learning about [Company] and the work the team is doing. After careful consideration, I've decided to respectfully decline the offer. The compensation package, at [$X], is significantly below both my current total compensation and the market rate I've researched for this type of role. Taking it would represent a meaningful step backward that I can't justify at this time. This decision was entirely about the economics of the offer — not about the role, the team, or the company, all of which I found compelling. I would genuinely welcome the opportunity to reconnect in the future if the compensation parameters change or if a different role opens up that's a closer fit on that dimension. I hope our paths cross again, and I wish the team every success with [project/product/initiative]. Best, [Your Name]
Key principles: Be clear that it's a decline (don't leave ambiguity). Cite the specific reason (compensation) without being confrontational. Keep the door explicitly open — companies often come back weeks or months later with better budget. End on genuine warmth.

Phrases to Use vs. Phrases to Avoid

The language you use in a salary negotiation email signals far more than the content. These specific phrases are used by successful negotiators — and the ones to avoid are the signals that make you look weak, entitled, or unprepared.

Context Use This Avoid This
Opening a counter "I've reviewed the offer carefully and was hoping we could discuss the base salary." "I need more money" or "The offer is too low"
Anchoring your ask "Based on market research for this role and location, I was targeting $X." "I was expecting more" or "My last job paid me $X"
Showing enthusiasm "I'm genuinely excited about this opportunity — I want to find a way to make it work." "I have other offers" (unless you actually do and are ready to use them)
Requesting flexibility "Is there flexibility in the base, or would a signing bonus be an option?" "Can you do anything?" or "Is that the best you can do?"
Citing credentials "Given my X years specifically in [domain] and the [specific skill/credential], I believe $X reflects the value I'd bring." "I'm worth more than this" or "I'm overqualified for the salary"
Responding to "no" "I understand. Would it be possible to revisit this at the 6-month mark?" Silence, or "Fine, I'll accept it then" immediately
Closing the negotiation "If we can reach $X, I'm ready to sign today." "I need to think about it" (without a specific decision timeline)
Discussing personal needs Never bring up personal financial needs — keep it market-based "I have student loans / a mortgage / kids" — these create sympathy, not leverage

What to Do When They Say No

A "no" to your salary counter-offer is not the end of the negotiation — it's the beginning of a different one. When an employer declines your initial counter, you have several moves available.

Ask What the Path to That Number Looks Like

Even if they can't meet your number today, ask what conditions would allow them to. "I understand that's not possible right now — what would the path look like to reach $X? Is there a performance review at 6 months? A promotion track?" This reframes the conversation from a yes/no to a roadmap, and sometimes produces a written commitment to revisit compensation at a specific milestone.

Pivot to Non-Salary Compensation

Many hiring managers have more latitude on one-time costs (signing bonuses) or non-cash benefits (remote work, additional PTO, professional development budget) than they do on base salary. A signing bonus of $10,000 is often easier to approve than a $10,000 increase in base salary because the base compounds into future raises, bonuses, and benefits, while the signing bonus is a one-time line item.

Ask for a Faster Performance Review

If the salary is truly fixed, request that your first performance review — with attached salary review — happen at 6 months rather than 12. This reduces your risk if you take the offer at a lower number and gets you to market faster if your performance warrants it.

Know When to Walk Away

If the gap between the offer and market rate is large and none of the alternatives close it adequately, it's completely professional to decline the offer and continue your search. An employer who can't or won't be competitive on compensation often signals broader issues with how they value their people.
