The 6-Step Process to Tailor Your Resume

1

Parse the Job Description for Keywords

Copy the full job description into a plain text document. Remove the "About Us" section, the benefits package, the EEOC boilerplate — everything except the Requirements, Responsibilities, and Qualifications sections. These are the sections that feed the ATS configuration, and these are the sections you need to mine.

Read through the cleaned text and highlight every noun and noun phrase that represents a skill, tool, methodology, certification, or qualification. Focus on nouns — ATS systems weight nouns far more heavily than verbs. "Manages cross-functional teams" contains one keyword: "cross-functional teams." That is what matters.

Count the frequency of each keyword. Terms that appear twice or three times in the job description are the recruiter's highest priorities — and the highest ATS scoring weight. Build a ranked list: tier 1 (appears 2+ times), tier 2 (appears once, in Requirements), tier 3 (appears once, in Preferred).

2

Audit Your Resume Against Those Keywords

Take your tier 1 and tier 2 keyword lists and compare them against your current resume, section by section. For each keyword, answer three questions: Is it present? Is it in the right form (exact phrase or close equivalent)? Is it in a high-weight section (summary, recent role bullets, skills)?

Mark each keyword as: present and well-placed (green), present but poorly placed or paraphrased (yellow), or absent (red). The yellow and red items are your work. The red items are your highest priority — these are keywords the ATS is looking for that your resume simply does not contain.

For a deeper dive into which keywords matter most by industry and role, see our guide: ATS Resume Keywords: The Complete List by Job Title (2026).

3

Rewrite Bullets to Incorporate Missing Keywords in Context

For each red and yellow keyword, identify an existing bullet point in your resume where you can legitimately introduce it. The keyword must reflect genuine experience — do not add skills you do not have. The question is whether there is a bullet that describes real work you did that could be rephrased to use the expected terminology.

The rewrite approach: keep the achievement and the numbers. Change the language describing the work to match the job description's vocabulary. If your bullet says "worked with product and design teams" and the job description says "cross-functional collaboration," rewrite the bullet to include "cross-functional collaboration" naturally — "Led cross-functional collaboration across product, design, and engineering to deliver [outcome]."

Aim to address your top 5–6 keywords through bullet rewrites. Prioritize your most recent role — those bullets carry the highest ATS weight. Older role bullets can be left largely untouched unless a critical keyword only fits there.

4

Update Your Professional Summary

Your professional summary is the highest-weighted section in ATS scoring. It is parsed first and receives the largest weight multiplier for keyword presence. A 3–4 sentence summary written for a generic resume misses a major opportunity to establish keyword context at the very start of the document.

Rewrite your summary to include your 4–5 most important tier-1 keywords from the job description — naturally, in real sentences that describe your professional identity. The summary should read like a strong executive introduction, not a keyword dump. "Results-driven marketing leader with 8 years of experience in demand generation, go-to-market strategy, and HubSpot-led marketing automation for B2B SaaS companies" hits five critical keywords in one sentence while reading professionally.

If you do not currently have a professional summary on your resume, add one. A 3–4 sentence summary is mandatory for ATS-optimized resumes in 2026.

5

Adjust Your Skills Section

Your skills section is the keyword catch-all — it is specifically designed to house the tools, technologies, certifications, and methodologies that ATS systems scan for directly. After updating your bullets and summary, review your skills section for any remaining keywords from your tier-1 and tier-2 lists that did not make it into the narrative sections.

Add each missing relevant skill directly to your skills section. Be specific: "CRM software" is not as valuable as "Salesforce CRM." "Cloud platforms" is not as valuable as "AWS, GCP, Azure." ATS systems score tool names as separate keywords — the more precisely you name them, the higher your match score.

Also review your current skills section for items that are not present in the job description. You do not need to remove them, but this review helps ensure the section is weighted toward relevance rather than comprehensiveness.

6

Score Your Resume Before Applying

Before you submit, get an ATS score. Sending in an unscored resume is like submitting a job application without proofreading — you might be fine, but you do not know. A score tells you whether you are in the shortlisted range (80+) or still in the filtered-out range (below 60).

Before and After: Real Bullet Rewrite Example

The most common question about tailoring is what it actually looks like in practice. Here is a real example of a marketing manager's bullet before and after tailoring for a demand generation role.

Job description keywords (tier 1): demand generation, HubSpot, pipeline generation, marketing automation, conversion rate optimization, MQL

Before — Generic
"Ran email marketing campaigns and managed our marketing software to improve the number of leads coming into the sales team each quarter."
After — Tailored
"Managed end-to-end demand generation campaigns via HubSpot marketing automation, increasing MQL volume 38% QoQ through targeted nurture sequences and landing page conversion rate optimization, directly contributing to pipeline generation growth of $2.1M."

The underlying work described in both bullets is the same. The candidate did not gain new skills between the before and after versions. What changed is the vocabulary — the after version uses the exact terminology the hiring team and ATS are looking for, and it includes a concrete result. Five tier-1 keywords are now present in a single high-impact bullet.

This is tailoring done right: not fabrication, not inflation, not keyword stuffing. It is accurate description of real work using the language that the employer expects to see.

What Tailoring Is Not: Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Copying job description sentences verbatim: Using exact phrases from the job description is fine. Copying entire sentences or paragraphs triggers ATS manipulation detection and will get you disqualified.
  • Adding skills you do not have: Listing a tool or skill you have never used because it appears in the job description will almost certainly come up in an interview — and it will end the conversation immediately. Only tailor using genuine experience.
  • Tailoring only the skills section: Many candidates update their skills list but leave their bullet points untouched. ATS systems weight keywords in experience bullets more heavily than in the skills section. Bullet tailoring is essential.
  • Using the same tailored resume for different roles: A resume tailored for one job should not be submitted to a different job without re-tailoring. The keywords that score well for a Senior Engineer role are different from those for a Staff Engineer role at the same company. Each application needs its own tailoring pass.
  • Neglecting the summary: Submitting without updating your summary means missing the highest-weighted keyword opportunity in the document. The summary is the first thing the ATS reads and carries the most weight per word of any section.
