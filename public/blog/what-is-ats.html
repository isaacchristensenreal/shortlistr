What is an ATS system? An Applicant Tracking System (ATS) is software used by employers to automate the collection, filtering, and ranking of job applications. When you submit a resume through a company's website, LinkedIn, or a job board, it almost always passes through an ATS before a human recruiter ever sees it. The ATS parses your resume into structured data, scores it against the job requirements, and either advances it to a recruiter or silently discards it. This process happens invisibly — you receive no notification either way.

Understanding how an ATS system works is the single most important piece of job search knowledge most candidates are missing. You can have a genuinely impressive background and still get filtered out because your resume uses the wrong word for a skill you absolutely have. The system is not evaluating your potential — it is matching text against criteria. Once you understand that, everything about resume optimization starts to make sense.

The core number: 75% of resumes are rejected by ATS before a human ever reads them. This is not a failure of qualifications — it is almost always a failure of formatting, keyword matching, or file structure that the ATS parser cannot correctly interpret.

How an ATS System Works: Step by Step

Most people imagine an ATS as a simple keyword search — the system looks for a word, either finds it or doesn't, and moves on. The reality is more sophisticated and, importantly, more beatable once you understand it. Here is exactly what happens from the moment you hit "Submit" to the moment a recruiter reviews (or doesn't review) your application.

  1. Application Ingestion

    Your resume file (PDF, DOCX, or other format) is uploaded to the ATS. The system strips all formatting — fonts, colors, columns, graphics — and converts your resume to raw plain text. This is the first place things can break: if your resume uses tables, text boxes, headers/footers, or columns, the parser may scramble the order of information or drop entire sections entirely.

  2. Parsing and Data Extraction

    The ATS parser reads your plain text and attempts to identify and categorize each piece of information: contact details, work history (company name, title, dates), education (institution, degree, graduation date), skills, certifications, and more. This data gets stored in structured fields in the ATS database. Parsing errors at this stage — caused by complex formatting — mean your information gets stored incorrectly or not at all.

  3. Keyword Matching and Scoring

    The recruiter has configured the ATS job posting with required and preferred keywords — typically the nouns and noun phrases from the job description. The system compares your parsed resume text against this keyword list and calculates a match score. Some systems weight keywords differently by location (title and summary rank higher than skills section). Most score both exact matches and, in newer systems, close semantic equivalents.

  4. Threshold Filtering

    Applications below a minimum score threshold are automatically moved to a "rejected" or "not reviewed" pile and never surface to a recruiter. The threshold varies by company and role, but is typically set somewhere between 60–75% match. This is the automated rejection step — it happens with no human involvement and no notification to the applicant.

  5. Ranking and Queue

    Applications that clear the threshold are ranked by score and presented to the recruiter in order. The recruiter typically sees the top 20–30% of applicants. A resume that scores 92% gets reviewed first; one that scores 71% may technically clear the threshold but sit so far down the queue it never gets seen before the role is filled.

  6. Human Review

    A recruiter or hiring manager reviews the applications that the ATS surfaced. At this point, they spend an average of 7 seconds on the initial scan. Only resumes that are both ATS-optimized and visually clean and readable make it to a phone screen. This is why ATS optimization and good resume design are not competing priorities — you need both.

Why 75% of Resumes Get Rejected Before a Human Sees Them

The 75% rejection statistic is not a myth or an exaggeration — it comes from research across multiple hiring platforms and is consistent with data from recruiters at high-volume hiring companies. Understanding the specific reasons resumes get filtered helps you avoid the most common traps.

Reason 1: Formatting That Breaks the Parser

Resumes built in design tools, templates with text boxes, or documents with multi-column layouts frequently break ATS parsers. The parser reads text in a linear order — left to right, top to bottom — and has no concept of two-column layouts. Content from the right column often gets appended to the end of the document or interleaved with left-column content, destroying the logical flow of your work history. Skills end up mixed in with job dates. Job titles appear next to the wrong companies. The ATS cannot make sense of the parsed output and your score craters.

Reason 2: Missing Keywords

The most common reason for ATS rejection is simply that a resume does not contain the specific terminology the job description uses. This is often not because the candidate lacks the relevant skills — it is because they describe those skills using different words. The candidate who has done "stakeholder alignment" but writes "relationship management" fails the ATS scan for that keyword. Exact phrase matching on older systems is unforgiving.

Reason 3: Wrong Job Titles

ATS systems frequently filter on job title as a hard criterion. If a job requires a "Product Manager" with 3+ years of experience and your title at your previous company was "Product Lead," you may be filtered out even if the roles were functionally identical. Using the industry-standard title variant that matches the job description — in your resume header and in your work history — closes this gap.

Reason 4: Unreadable File Formats

Submitting a resume as a Canva PDF, a scanned document, a .pages file, or an image-based PDF means the ATS receives either corrupted text or no text at all. Your resume is scored as a blank document and is immediately filtered out. Always submit a text-based PDF or a .docx file.

Reason 5: Missing Contact Information Sections

Some ATS systems use the presence of contact information as a basic validity check. If your phone number, email, or location cannot be parsed — often because they are in a header/footer or a design element — the system may discard the application as incomplete. Contact information should always appear in the main body of the document, in plain text.

The invisible rejection problem

When an ATS rejects your resume, you almost never find out. You receive either a generic "we'll keep your application on file" auto-response or nothing at all. The result is that most candidates spend months sending out the same resume — the one that keeps getting filtered — without ever knowing why. The rejection isn't a judgment of their abilities. It is a parsing failure. And it is almost always fixable.

What ATS Systems Are Specifically Looking For

Beyond the general mechanics, it helps to know precisely which elements of your resume carry the most weight in ATS scoring. Here is a breakdown of the four primary factors.

1. Keywords and Keyword Placement

Keywords are the primary scoring mechanism. The most important keywords to match are the role-specific nouns from the job description: job titles, hard skills, tools, certifications, methodologies, and industry-specific terms. ATS systems weight keyword placement: a keyword in your professional title or summary is worth more than the same keyword in your skills section, which is worth more than a mention in an old job from five years ago.

For a deep dive into which keywords matter most by job category, see our guide: ATS Resume Keywords: The Complete List by Job Title (2026).

2. Formatting and Parseability

A resume that the ATS cannot correctly parse is effectively invisible. The formatting rules for ATS compatibility are specific:

  • Single-column layout — multi-column layouts break most parsers
  • Standard section headers — "Work Experience," "Education," "Skills" are reliably recognized; creative headers like "Where I've Made an Impact" are not
  • No text boxes or tables — content inside these elements is often skipped entirely
  • No headers or footers — contact information placed in document headers is frequently ignored
  • Standard fonts — unusual fonts may be substituted or garbled in the parsed output
  • No graphics or icons — purely decorative; invisible to text parsers

3. File Type

A .docx file created in Microsoft Word is the most universally parseable format across all ATS platforms. A text-based PDF from Word or Google Docs is fine for most modern systems. Avoid design-generated PDFs, scanned documents, and any format other than .docx or a clean PDF.

4. Completeness of Key Sections

ATS parsers expect to find specific sections in a resume. Missing sections — or sections with non-standard titles — can cause the system to assign a low completeness score. Required sections in most ATS configurations: contact information, professional summary or objective, work experience (with dates), education (with dates and institution), and skills. Optional but frequently scored: certifications, professional development, and LinkedIn URL.

How to Optimize Your Resume for ATS: A Practical Checklist

Knowing how an ATS works is only useful if it changes how you write and format your resume. Here is the complete ATS optimization checklist — the exact steps that move a resume from filtered to shortlisted.

  • Use a single-column, plain layout — no tables, no columns, no text boxes, no graphics
  • Submit as .docx or text-based PDF — never a Canva PDF or scanned image
  • Put contact info in the document body — not in document headers/footers
  • Use standard section titles — "Work Experience," "Education," "Skills," "Certifications"
  • Mirror the job title from the posting — in your resume header and most recent role
  • Include an ATS-friendly summary section — 3–4 sentences containing your 5–6 most important keywords
  • Use exact phrases from the job description — not paraphrased equivalents, especially for older ATS platforms
  • Include each critical keyword 2–3 times — in summary, in experience bullets, and in skills section
  • Spell out abbreviations on first use — "Continuous Integration/Continuous Deployment (CI/CD)"
  • Add a dedicated skills section — list tools, technologies, certifications, and methodologies explicitly
  • Include dates for all positions — month and year format; missing dates trigger parsing errors
  • Score your resume before submitting — use a tool like ShortListr to see your ATS match score before you apply

The most important insight from this checklist: ATS optimization and human readability are not in conflict. A clean, single-column resume with clear section headers and keyword-rich bullets is both highly ATS-compatible and easy for a human to scan. The resumes that fail ATS — the graphic-heavy, multi-column, creatively-sectioned designs — also tend to be harder for humans to read quickly. The optimal resume serves both audiences.
