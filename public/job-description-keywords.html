Keyword discovery

Where Keywords Hide in a Job Description

Not all parts of a job posting carry equal weight. ATS systems and recruiters focus on specific sections — and so should you when extracting keywords.

The job title itself

The exact title is often a required keyword. "Product Manager" and "Senior Product Manager" are scored separately — match the title as closely as you can in your resume headline.

Required qualifications

The single most important section. Every bullet here is a non-negotiable keyword signal. Pull every tool, technology, certification, and skill listed — these are baseline ATS filters.

Preferred qualifications

Preferred keywords that genuinely apply to your background are still worth including — they increase your score above the baseline. Don't fabricate, but don't skip them if they're real.

Responsibilities section

Responsibilities reveal what the role actually does day-to-day — and the verbs used here (led, managed, built, analyzed) are the action keywords your bullets should mirror.

Repeated words

Any phrase that appears more than once is a high-priority keyword. Frequency is a proxy for importance — both to the recruiter writing the job post and to the ATS scoring your resume.

Company about section

The company description often contains industry-specific language and culture keywords that align with the role. Terms like "startup environment" or "enterprise scale" can signal relevant context keywords.

Keyword categories

Hard Skills vs. Soft Skills vs. ATS Triggers

Keywords aren't all the same. Understanding the three categories helps you prioritize what to include — and where to put it in your resume.

Hard Skills

Highest ATS weight

Specific, verifiable tools, technologies, methodologies, and credentials. These are what ATS systems filter on most aggressively.

Python Salesforce PMP SQL React Google Analytics HubSpot AWS
Soft Skills

Lower ATS weight, recruiter-read

Behavioral and interpersonal traits. Less heavily weighted by ATS but important for the human who reads your resume after it passes the filter.

cross-functional communication leadership collaboration problem-solving adaptability
ATS Triggers

Binary pass/fail filters

Phrases that act as hard cutoffs — not keyword scores but pass/fail gates. Missing these can eliminate you regardless of your overall match rate.

5+ years experience Bachelor's degree authorized to work U.S. based remote eligible

Prioritization rule: Get every hard skill keyword first — these are your ATS filters. Then handle ATS triggers (especially experience years and degree requirements). Soft skills come last and should appear in experience bullets, not a standalone list.

How to Use Keywords Without It Sounding Robotic

The goal isn't to stuff keywords into your resume — it's to describe your real experience using the vocabulary the employer uses. Here's how to do that without it reading like a keyword list.

  • 1

    Put keywords in experience bullets, not just a skills section

    ATS systems weight keywords in context — specifically in your work experience — more heavily than a skills list. "Used Salesforce to manage 200-account pipeline" scores higher than "Salesforce" in a skills row.

  • 2

    Mirror exact phrasing — not synonyms

    If the job says "stakeholder management," don't write "managing stakeholders." ATS matches strings. "Machine Learning" and "ML" may score differently depending on the system. Use the exact form from the job description.

  • 3

    Lead bullets with keywords, not filler phrases

    Start bullets with an action verb followed by the keyword. Avoid openers like "Responsible for…" or "Helped with…" — they bury the keyword and weaken the sentence for human readers too.

  • 4

    Add keywords to your summary/headline too

    Your professional summary is parsed first. Include your target job title and 2–3 top hard skill keywords here — it sets the context for everything that follows and can boost your opening ATS score.

  • 5

    Never invent experience — contextualize real experience differently

    If you have the skill but described it with different words, the fix is rewording — not fabricating. You're not adding fake experience; you're making your real experience legible to the system reading it.

The manual method

The 7-Step Keyword Extraction Process

If you want to do this manually for a specific role, here's the exact process to follow:

1

Read the job title and opening paragraph first

The title and first 2–3 sentences contain the role's highest-priority keywords. Every noun and action verb in the opening is a signal. Note them before reading anything else.

2

Copy the entire required qualifications section

Paste it into a separate document. Every tool, technology, credential, and skill listed here is a non-negotiable keyword. Nothing in this section should be absent from your resume if it applies to you.

3

Highlight every word that appears more than once

Use Ctrl+F to find repeated terms. Any phrase appearing twice or more carries elevated ATS weight. Frequency is a direct signal of priority.

4

Separate keywords into three buckets

Hard skills (tools, technologies, certifications), soft skills (leadership, communication, collaboration), and ATS triggers (years of experience, degree requirements, location). Prioritize in that order.

5

Match each keyword to your actual experience

For each keyword, identify where in your work history you used it. If you haven't, skip it honestly. If you did but described it differently, note the translation.

6

Rewrite your experience bullets to include keywords in context

Update your resume so each keyword is woven into an achievement-oriented bullet. Use the exact phrasing from the job description, not paraphrased versions.

7

Run your resume through an ATS scanner before submitting

Common questions

Frequently Asked Questions

  • Which keywords in a job description matter most for ATS?
    The highest-priority keywords are in the required qualifications section and appear more than once in the posting. Job title keywords, specific tools/technologies, and certifications tend to have the highest ATS scoring weight.
  • Should I copy keywords exactly from the job description?
    Yes — mirror the exact phrasing used in the job posting. If the job says "project management" don't write "managing projects." ATS systems match exact strings, so the specific capitalization and word order can affect your score.
  • Can I just add a keywords section to the bottom of my resume?
    A skills section helps, but modern ATS systems weight keywords found in your experience bullets more heavily than standalone lists. Keywords in context — describing where and how you used the skill — score better.
  • How many keywords should I include from a job description?
    Target 100% of the required qualifications keywords and as many preferred keywords as honestly apply to you. An ATS match rate of 65–80% on required keywords is a strong baseline to aim for.
  • Is keyword stuffing bad for ATS?
    Keyword stuffing — listing words out of context, hiding white text, or artificially inflating keyword density — can trigger spam filters in some ATS systems and will immediately disqualify you when a recruiter reads the resume. Always integrate keywords naturally into real experience descriptions.
