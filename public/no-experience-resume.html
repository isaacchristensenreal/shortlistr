What ATS Scores When Experience Is Thin

ATS Doesn't Know You're a Beginner — It Only Knows Keywords

This is the most important thing to understand about ATS and entry-level resumes: the system is not aware of your career stage. It parses text and checks for matches. That means keyword placement is everything — and you have more places to put keywords than you think.

Format Rules That Boost Score

The Formatting Decisions That Change Your ATS Score Regardless of Experience

Two resumes with identical content can score very differently based on format alone. These rules apply to every candidate — but beginners are most likely to get them wrong because they're relying on design-forward templates.

✓ Format choices that help
  • Single-column layout — ATS reads top to bottom, left to right
  • Standard section headers: Education, Experience, Projects, Skills
  • Plain bullet points — no icons, stars, or custom symbols
  • Contact info in the body, not a designed header block
  • Hard skills listed as plain text in a Skills section
  • Relevant Coursework listed under Education as a comma-separated line
  • Project titles that mirror industry terminology
  • Saved as .docx or a text-based PDF — not an image
  • 11–12pt font; 0.5–1" margins; standard typeface
  • One page — always, with no experience
✗ Format choices that hurt
  • Two-column or sidebar layout (extremely common in Canva templates)
  • Tables used to organize skills or work history
  • Progress bars or rating scales for skills (meaningless and unreadable by ATS)
  • Name and contact info placed inside a designed graphic header
  • Functional resume format organized by skill category — always score poorly
  • Colorful section dividers or shaded background blocks
  • Text boxes or floating callout sections
  • Logos, icons, or headshot photos
  • Objective statement that's generic ("motivated team player seeking opportunity")
  • Saving as PNG, JPG, or image-based PDF
