What the Test Covers

Six Things the ATS Test Checks in 30 Seconds

The test doesn't give you a vague score — it checks six specific categories, returns individual scores for each, and tells you what to fix in priority order.

Check 01

Keyword Match Score

The core ATS metric. Your resume text is compared word-by-word against the job description. Each keyword in the JD that's missing from your resume lowers your score. The test shows your overall match percentage and every missing term.

Check 02

Format & Parseability

ATS can fail to read multi-column layouts, tables, text boxes, and image-based PDFs — scrambling your content before it's even scored. The test detects these format risks and flags exactly which elements are causing them.

Check 03

Skills Section Coverage

ATS scans for a dedicated Skills section and scores hard skills listed there against the JD. Missing the section entirely — or listing the wrong terms — can cost significant points even if skills appear in your bullets.

Check 04

Experience Language Strength

How well does the language in your experience bullets align with the language in the JD? Generic verbs and vague descriptions score lower than role-specific terms. The test shows which bullets have low keyword density.

Check 05

Section Structure

ATS expects standard section headers: Experience, Education, Skills, Summary. Non-standard headers ("What I've Done," "My Story") confuse parsers. The test checks that your sections are labeled in ways ATS recognizes.

Check 06

File Compatibility

Text-based PDF and .docx are safe. Image PDFs, .png, and scanned documents score zero — ATS can't extract text. The test confirms your file type is readable and flags any compatibility issues before you apply.

After the Test

What to Do When Your Score Comes Back Low

A low score isn't a rejection — it's a to-do list. Here's the exact sequence to follow, in order of impact. Most resumes can move from 30% to 70%+ in under 20 minutes.

1

Add a plain-text Skills section if you don't have one

This is the single highest-impact change for low scorers. A Skills section gives ATS a concentrated list of hard keywords to match. List tools, technologies, and hard skills from the JD — exact phrasing, comma-separated, no tables or icons.

+15–25 pts typical impact
2

Work through the missing keyword list top-to-bottom

Shortlistr gives you the specific keywords that are missing. For each one: check whether it belongs in your Skills section (hard skills and tools) or in an experience bullet (role-specific terms and methodologies). Add each where it's accurate.

+10–20 pts typical impact
3

Rewrite your summary with the top 4–5 missing keywords

ATS scores the summary section first and weights it heavily. If your summary doesn't use the job title and core skill terms from the JD, you're losing easy points. A targeted 2–3 sentence summary with role-specific vocabulary can add significant score points quickly.

+8–15 pts typical impact
4

Fix any format issues flagged by the test

If the test flagged a two-column layout, tables, or contact info in a header block — fix those before resubmitting. Format issues can cause ATS to score incorrectly regardless of how many keywords you add. A single-column layout with all content in the main body is always safest.

Prevents score floor issues
5

Re-run the test and check your new score

Paste your updated resume back into Shortlistr against the same JD. Your score should move meaningfully — if it doesn't, check whether you're using the exact phrasing from the JD (not synonyms), and whether the format issues have been resolved so ATS can read the new content.

Target: 70%+ before applying
6

Repeat for every application — don't reuse the same version

Each job description is different. A resume that scores 78% on one marketing role may score 41% on a slightly different one at another company. Run the test for each posting you care about. You're editing a base document — it takes 10 minutes, not starting over.

Compounds over time
