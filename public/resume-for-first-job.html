Ideal First Resume Structure

What Your Resume Should Look Like

Click any section below to see why it matters for ATS and what to include. The order here is intentional — lead with your strengths.

Contact & Header

Name, email, phone, LinkedIn, city. Keep it plain text — no tables, no columns. ATS skips content in text boxes or graphic headers.

Summary / Objective

2–3 sentences tailored to the role. Pack in job-title keywords and 2–3 relevant skills. This is the first thing ATS scores — make it count.

Education (lead here)

Degree, school, graduation year. Add a "Relevant Coursework" line with course names that match job description keywords — ATS reads them.

Projects & Experience

Your most powerful keyword source. Describe capstone projects, internships, freelance work, and class assignments with measurable outcomes.

Skills

A plain-text list of hard skills — tools, languages, platforms, certifications. ATS keyword-scans this section heavily. Mirror exact terms from the JD.

Activities & Awards

Leadership roles, clubs, honors, volunteer work. Useful for soft-skill keywords and showing initiative. Keep it brief — 2–4 lines max.

ATS & Entry-Level Resumes

What ATS Scans When You Have No Experience

ATS doesn't know you're a first-time applicant. It scans everything on your resume for keyword matches against the job description. Here's where those matches come from.

💡

The good news: ATS doesn't require paid work experience. It requires keyword alignment. That means a well-written first resume with the right terms from your education and projects can score just as high as a candidate with two years of experience who didn't tailor their resume.

🎓

Education & Coursework

Your degree title, major, and relevant course names are all scannable text. Course names often directly mirror job description keywords — especially in technical and business roles.

Data Structures Financial Modeling Marketing Analytics UX Research
💻

Projects & Capstones

Class projects, personal projects, and capstone work are treated the same as job experience by ATS. Describe them with the same action-verb + result structure — and name the tools explicitly.

Python SQL Tableau A/B testing
🛠️

Skills Section

A dedicated Skills section is your most efficient ATS tool. List hard skills — tools, platforms, languages — as plain comma-separated text. ATS keyword-matches directly against this list.

Salesforce Google Analytics Figma Excel
🏆

Activities & Leadership

Club leadership, volunteer roles, and academic awards carry soft-skill keywords: "team leadership," "project management," "client communication." These matter when JDs require them.

How to Write Bullets That Score High

The difference between a bullet ATS ignores and one it scores isn't work experience — it's specificity. Here's the same entry-level experience written two ways.

✗ Low ATS score · ~28%
Helped with a group project on customer data
Did research for marketing class assignment
Used Excel for finance homework
Vague language. No tool names. No outcomes. ATS can't match these against a job description for a data analyst role.
✓ High ATS score · ~79%
Analyzed 3,000-row customer dataset using Python and Pandas; identified 4 churn predictors for capstone presentation
Conducted competitive market analysis using Google Analytics and SEMrush; presented findings to class of 24 peers
Built dynamic financial model in Excel with pivot tables and VLOOKUP; projected revenue scenarios for 3 growth paths
Exact tool names, quantified scope, outcome language. Every underlined term is a potential keyword match against a data/marketing JD.
Format Rules for First Resumes

Format Changes That Help You Rank Higher

First-time job seekers often use design-heavy templates that look impressive but parse terribly. These rules apply regardless of experience level.

✓ Do this
  • Single-column layout top to bottom
  • Standard section headers: Education, Skills, Experience, Projects
  • Plain text bullet points with a simple dash or circle
  • 11–12pt font, standard typeface (Arial, Calibri, Georgia)
  • Contact info in main body — not the header/footer zone
  • Save as .docx or text-based PDF (not a scanned image)
  • Lead with Education when you have limited work history
  • Include a Skills section with exact tool names from the JD
✗ Avoid this
  • Two-column or side-bar layouts (very common in Canva templates)
  • Tables used to organize skills or experience
  • Icons, graphics, or progress bars for skills
  • Name or contact info in a designed header box
  • Colored text sections or shaded background blocks
  • "Creative" resume templates from design sites
  • Objective statements that are generic ("seeking a challenging role")
  • Saving as an image PDF or .png file
